Mattancherry Palace - Historic Dutch Palace in Kochi

A magnificent example of Kerala architecture with Portuguese influences

About Mattancherry Palace

The Mattancherry Palace, also known as the Dutch Palace, is a Portuguese palace in Mattancherry, Kochi, in the Indian state of Kerala. The palace was built and gifted by the Portuguese as a present to the King of Cochin around 1555. It is a fine example of the Kerala style of architecture interspersed with colonial influences.

The palace was built to appease the king after they plundered a temple nearby. The Dutch carried out some extensions and renovations in the palace in 1663, and thereafter it was popularly called the Dutch Palace. The palace is a two-story structure built in a quadrangular format with a courtyard containing a temple dedicated to the royal family's protective goddess.

Built In

1555 AD (Portuguese), renovated 1663 (Dutch)

Mural paintings depicting Hindu epics

Mattancherry, Kochi, Kerala

Architectural Marvel

The Mattancherry Palace is a two-storied structure built in the traditional Kerala style known as Nalukettu, with four separate wings opening into a central courtyard. The courtyard contains a small temple dedicated to Pazhayannur Bhagavati, the protective goddess of the Kochi royal family.

The palace features low, sloping roofs with overhanging eaves, thick walls, and massive wooden doorsâ€”all characteristic of Kerala architecture. The interior is notable for its magnificent murals depicting scenes from the Hindu epics Ramayana and Mahabharata, as well as legends from the Puranas.

Historical Significance

The palace was built by the Portuguese and presented to Veera Kerala Varma, the Raja of Kochi, in 1555. The Dutch renovated the palace in 1663, giving it its current name. The palace was the official residence of the kings of Kochi until 1951 when the last Raja moved to a new palace.

In 1951, the palace was declared a protected monument by the Government of Kerala and was subsequently taken over by the Archaeological Survey of India. Today, it functions as a museum showcasing the history of the former Kingdom of Cochin, with portraits of the Rajas, royal costumes, palanquins, weapons, and other artifacts.

Visitor Information

The palace is open from 10:00 AM to 5:00 PM, closed on Fridays. There is a nominal entry fee for visitors. Photography is prohibited inside the palace to protect the delicate murals. The best time to visit is from October to March when the weather in Kochi is pleasant. Guided tours are available for those interested in learning about the palace's rich history.
